Key Findings The vulnerability stems from the failure to forward the approver's security scopes (callerScopes) in the /pair approve command from extensions/device-pair/index.ts to the core approval logic in src/infra/device-pairing.ts. As a result, the authorization model intended to ensure that users can only grant scopes they themselves possess is bypassed. Any user or device with the lowest privilege (operator.pairing) can approve a new device request with the highest privilege (operator.admin), resulting in silent and complete instance takeover. The issue is rated CVSS 8.6 (HIGH) and affects all OpenClaw versions before 2026.3.28, including all 2026.2.x and early 2026.3.x releases. The recommended and only effective remediation is to update to OpenClaw 2026.3.28 or later, as no configuration-based workaround exists. The vulnerability is particularly dangerous in the majority of deployments (over 63%) operating without authentication, allowing attackers to obtain pairing access without credentials. Indicators of Compromise (IOCs) Compromise can be detected by reviewing device lists and audit logs for suspicious activity. Specifically, the presence of devices with operator.admin scope that were approved by accounts holding only operator.pairing rights, especially if approvals occurred after the last upgrade to a non-patched version, strongly indicates exploitation. Audit logs should be inspected for /pair approve events, focusing on cases where approvals are performed by non-admin users, and where registration and approval timestamps are close together, indicative of automated or attacker-driven activity. Any unexplained admin device, especially on unauthenticated or recently unpatched instances, is a critical IOC. Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures (TTPs) The attack chain for CVE-2026-33579 is straightforward and low-complexity. First, the attacker acquires operator.pairing access—trivially on unauthenticated systems, or by compromising a legitimate low-privilege account. Next, the attacker submits a device pairing request specifying operator.admin as the desired scope. Because the authorization logic does not verify that the approver has sufficient privileges, the attacker (using their low-privilege account) issues the /pair approve command. The vulnerable logic then grants the requested admin privileges to the attacker's device, resulting in full control. No user interaction beyond the initial pairing is required, and no secondary exploits are necessary. On systems with authentication enabled, the attack is gated by the need to compromise a pairing-scoped account, but on the majority of deployments with authentication disabled, privilege escalation is virtually unauthenticated.
